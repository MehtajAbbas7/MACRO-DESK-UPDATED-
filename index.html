Federal Reserve · Rates

Warsh's first FOMC turns hawkish as inflation reaccelerates to 4.2%

Macro Desk Briefing · Sourced from Reuters, Bloomberg, BLS, CME FedWatch

The US economy is facing uncertainty due to geopolitical tensions and regulatory developments, with the recent block on the Trump administration's limits on student loan forgiveness program adding to the mix. The rollout of OpenAI's GPT-5.6 has also been approved, which could have significant implications for the tech industry. Overall, these factors are contributing to a complex and volatile macro environment.

That's a sharp reversal from the consensus going into 2026, when most major banks — Goldman, JPMorgan, MUFG among them — were positioned for a weaker dollar on the assumption the Fed would keep cutting. Those calls were conditional on disinflation continuing. It hasn't. Warsh has so far declined to commit to a forward path, but his repeated emphasis on "price stability" in the press conference read as hawkish to a market that had been pricing dovishness.

"Every bearish-dollar bank target on the street this year rested on the same assumption: that the Fed cuts. With inflation at 4.2%, that assumption is the thing that broke."

The knock-on effects are showing up everywhere you'd expect. The dollar index is holding near one-year highs around 100.8. Gold has lost its safe-haven bid to rate-driven selling, dropping roughly 25% from January's all-time high of $5,589 even as the Iran conflict persists — a genuinely unusual setup, since the same war that should support gold is also the inflation source keeping the Fed restrictive. And US equities have shrugged it off almost entirely, with the S&P riding strong May jobs data and AI capex enthusiasm to within range of fresh highs.

The four forces driving sentiment right nowRanked by current weight

Fed policy path

USD bullish

This is the dominant driver of everything else on this page. A new, untested Fed chair, a committee split roughly down the middle on hiking, and inflation running well above target together remove the one variable (rate cuts) that every dollar-bearish forecast depended on.

Why it moves price: higher expected rates make holding dollars more attractive relative to other currencies (rate differential), and make non-yielding gold less attractive to hold.

Inflation (CPI / PCE)

Mixed, leaning hawkish

May CPI at 4.2% headline, 2.9% core. The gap between the two tells the real story: this isn't broad demand-driven inflation, it's an energy-price shock from the Iran conflict feeding through to headline numbers. Core staying contained is the one thing keeping this from being a full-blown crisis print.

Why it moves price: headline CPI is what the Fed and markets react to fastest, even when the underlying (core) picture is more nuanced — so this print outweighs its "true" signal.

Labour market

USD bullish

May non-farm payrolls printed 172,000 against expectations of roughly 80–85,000 — a beat large enough to materially shift rate-hike odds higher within hours. A resilient labour market removes the Fed's main justification for cutting, since "support the labour market" was the dovish case's foundation.

Why it moves price: a strong jobs market gives the Fed room to stay restrictive without risking a recession narrative, which is dollar-supportive and gold/equity-neutral-to-negative in the short run.

Iran conflict / geopolitical risk

Gold's dilemma

Normally a war that threatens Gulf oil supply is straightforwardly gold-bullish — safe-haven demand surges. This time it's structurally different: the conflict is driving an energy-price spike that's pushing inflation higher, which keeps the Fed hawkish, which makes holding non-yielding gold more expensive. The safe-haven bid and the rate-driven headwind are fighting each other.

Why it moves price: gold needs either a ceasefire (removing the inflation channel, freeing the Fed to cut) or a sharp escalation (overwhelming the rate story with pure fear) to break decisively in either direction. Right now it's stuck between the two.

Intermarket cheatsheetHow these assets typically move together

DXY vs Gold ▸

Usually strongly inverse — a stronger dollar makes gold (priced in USD) more expensive for foreign buyers, dampening demand. Watch for this relationship to break down during acute geopolitical stress, like now, when gold's safe-haven bid fights the dollar's rate-driven strength.

US 10Y yield vs Gold ▸

Usually inverse — rising real yields increase the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding gold. This is the single cleanest read on gold's rate sensitivity, often clearer than watching DXY directly.

Oil vs Inflation expectations ▸

Energy is a direct CPI input, so oil spikes feed into headline inflation within weeks. With the Iran conflict ongoing, this is currently one of the most important charts to watch — it's the transmission line from geopolitics to Fed policy.

Risk sentiment vs JPY / CHF ▸

Yen and franc are traditional funding/safe-haven currencies. They tend to strengthen when equities sell off sharply (risk-off) and weaken when risk appetite is high, somewhat independent of their own domestic data.

FTSE 100 vs GBP ▸

Often inverse, unusually — many FTSE 100 companies earn revenue abroad, so a weaker pound can boost their reported (translated) earnings, pushing the index up even as sterling falls.

S&P 500 vs Nasdaq divergence ▸

When these diverge sharply, it's usually a tech/AI-capex-specific story (rates, chip demand, single-stock earnings) rather than a broad macro one — useful for telling apart a market-wide move from a sector rotation.

Why does CPI move the dollar at all? ▸

Higher inflation pressures the Fed to keep interest rates higher for longer (or hike). Higher rates make dollar-denominated assets pay more to hold, pulling in foreign capital and pushing demand for USD up — strengthening it relative to currencies from countries with lower rates.

Why is gold "non-yielding" — and why does that matter? ▸

Gold pays no interest or dividend just for holding it. When rates rise, the opportunity cost of holding gold instead of a bond paying 4-5% goes up — so capital rotates out of gold into yield-bearing assets. That's why hawkish Fed news is usually gold-negative, even amid uncertainty.

What's a "dot plot" and why do traders obsess over it? ▸

It's an anonymous chart of where each of the Fed's 18 policymakers thinks rates should be at future meetings. Markets use the median dot to estimate the most likely policy path — a shift in the dots (even with no rate change announced) can move markets as much as an actual decision.

Why can a war be both gold-bullish and gold-bearish? ▸

Wars usually trigger safe-haven buying (bullish for gold). But if the war disrupts energy supply, it also pushes inflation up, which can force central banks to keep rates higher — and higher rates pull money out of gold. The net effect depends on which force is stronger at a given moment.

Why does a strong jobs report sometimes hurt stocks/gold? ▸

A "good" economic number can be read as bad news for rate-sensitive assets, because it reduces the chance the Fed needs to cut rates to support the economy. Markets often price in future Fed moves more than the present state of the economy.

What does "DXY" actually measure? ▸

The Dollar Index tracks USD against a basket of six currencies, weighted mostly by the euro (57.6%), then yen, pound, Canadian dollar, krona, and franc. It rises when USD strengthens against that basket on average — even if it's weak against one currency in isolation.

Why this gets its own section

Unscheduled statements from key figures — the Fed chair, the White House, central bank heads, Iranian officials — move markets faster than scheduled data because there's no time to price them in advance. This section pulls anything from a market-moving voice, not just one person or one conflict.

Watch for: escalation / hawkish language

Risk-off / rate-up trigger

Strike threats, Hormuz mentions, troop movements, rejected ceasefire terms on the geopolitical side — or unexpectedly hawkish Fed/central bank language on the policy side. Both push in a similar direction: higher uncertainty or higher rate expectations.

Why it moves price: geopolitical escalation feeds oil-driven inflation; hawkish policy language feeds rate expectations directly. Either can spike USD and pressure risk assets.

Watch for: de-escalation / dovish language

Relief trigger

Ceasefire signing, troop withdrawal, sanctions relief talk — or a Fed/central bank official softening on rates. Either could unwind the current hawkish-Fed, energy-shock narrative driving this whole page.

Why it moves price: removes either the inflation channel (geopolitics) or the rate channel (policy) currently keeping USD bid and gold capped.

Glossary — every term you'll see on this pageSearch or scroll

CPI ▸

Consumer Price Index — measures how much prices for everyday goods/services have changed. The headline inflation number markets and central banks react to most.

Core CPI / PCE ▸

Inflation excluding volatile food and energy prices. Considered a cleaner read on underlying inflation trends than the headline number.

PCE ▸

Personal Consumption Expenditures — the Fed's officially preferred inflation gauge, slightly different methodology to CPI, usually runs a bit lower.

NFP (Non-Farm Payrolls) ▸

Monthly US jobs report excluding farm workers, government, and a few other categories. One of the most market-moving releases every month.

Dot plot ▸

An anonymous chart showing where each of the Fed's 18 officials expects interest rates to be at future meetings. The median dot signals the likely policy path.

FOMC ▸

Federal Open Market Committee — the Fed group that actually votes on interest rate decisions. Meets eight times a year.

Hawkish / Dovish ▸

Hawkish means leaning toward higher rates / tighter policy (usually USD-bullish). Dovish means leaning toward lower rates / looser policy (usually USD-bearish).

Rate differential ▸

The gap between two countries' interest rates. Money tends to flow toward the currency offering the higher rate, all else equal — a core driver of FX moves.

DXY (Dollar Index) ▸

Tracks the US dollar against a basket of six currencies, weighted mostly toward the euro (57.6%). Rises when USD strengthens against that basket on average.

Yield (bond yield) ▸

The return an investor earns for holding a government bond. Rising yields usually pull money out of non-yielding assets like gold, and can pressure stocks by raising the "risk-free" comparison rate.

Real yield ▸

Bond yield minus inflation. This is what actually matters for gold — even a high nominal yield isn't gold-bearish if inflation is eating all of it.

Safe haven ▸

An asset investors flock to during fear or uncertainty — gold, USD, JPY, CHF, and US Treasuries are the classic examples.

Risk-on / Risk-off ▸

Risk-on means investors are comfortable taking risk (buying stocks, selling havens). Risk-off means the opposite — fear dominates, money rotates into safety.

Quantitative easing / tightening (QE/QT) ▸

QE: central bank buys assets to inject money into the system, pushing rates down. QT: the reverse — letting those assets run off, tightening financial conditions.

Basis point (bp) ▸

One hundredth of a percentage point. A "25bp hike" means a 0.25% increase in rates.

GDP ▸

Gross Domestic Product — total value of everything a country produces. The broadest measure of economic growth or contraction.

Recession ▸

Commonly defined as two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth, though official calls (e.g. in the US) weigh broader data too.

DXY-weighted currencies ▸

EUR (57.6%), JPY (13.6%), GBP (11.9%), CAD (9.1%), SEK (4.2%), CHF (3.6%) — the six currencies that make up the dollar index basket.

Central bank divergence ▸

When two central banks move policy in opposite directions (one hiking, one cutting) — usually creates the cleanest, strongest FX trends.

Forward guidance ▸

A central bank signalling its likely future policy moves in advance, to shape market expectations before anything actually changes.

Carry trade ▸

Borrowing in a low-rate currency (like JPY) to invest in a higher-rate one, pocketing the difference. Unwinds violently during risk-off shocks.

OPEC+ ▸

The oil producers' cartel (OPEC plus allies like Russia) that coordinates output to influence oil prices — a key input into global inflation.

Terminal rate ▸

The level markets expect a central bank's rate-hiking or cutting cycle to eventually settle at — the "final destination" rate.

Stagflation ▸

The uncomfortable combination of high inflation and weak/no growth — the hardest environment for a central bank to navigate, since fixing one usually worsens the other.
